<p>Not "яаблоки", but "яблоки". Singular is "яблоко".</p>
<p>Always look at the noun's ending. If the last letter is  "и" or "ы" (in nominative case), that's plural!</p>
<p><em>Река́ — реки (river — rivers), singular feminine ending "а" transforms into "и";</em></p>
<p><em>лиса́ — ли́сы (fox — foxes), singular feminine ending "а" transforms into "ы";</em></p>
<p><em>стол — столы́ (table — tables), singular masculine zero-ending transforms into "ы";</em></p>
<p><em>сок — со́ки (juice — juices), singular masculine zero-ending transforms into "и".</em></p>
<p>Of course, there are exceptions, where you can find neither "и" nor "ы". For example:</p>
<p><em>мо́ре — моря́ (sea — seas), singular neutral ending "е" transforms into "я";</em></p>
<p><em>по́ле — поля́ (field — fields), singular neutral ending "е" transforms into "я";</em></p>
<p><em>лист — ли́стья (leaf — leaves), singular masculine zero-ending transforms into "я";</em></p>
<p><em>зе́ркало —  зеркала́ (mirror — mirrors), singular neutral ending "о" transforms into "а́";</em></p>
<p><em>ребёнок — де́ти (child — children), — "ребёнок" hasn't plural form, you can't say "ребёнки", so we use the word "дети" that hasn't singular form in modern language (though there is pretty ancient "дитя́")</em></p>
<p><em>челове́к — лю́ди (human — people),</em></p>
<p><em>такси́ — такси́ (taxi) — that's a loan word, it doesn't change its form ever.</em></p>

<p>тридцать два яблока</p>
<p>пять яблок</p>
<p>тринадцать яблок</p>
<p>With the letter К, you wouldn't use Ы, because the sound "кы" is not really used in Russian.</p></div>

<div class="_3e8fY _2povu" itemprop="text"><p>Ты means "you," when you're talking to one person, whom you know familiarly, like a friend or relative, or to a child or minor. Same with a boss talking to their subordinates.</p>
<p>Вы means "you" when you're talking to one person with whom you're not familiar and speaking formally, or an elderly person or family member. A child is always supposed to refer to adults outside of his family as "вы." A subordinate would call his boss "вы."</p>
<p>Вы also means "you" when you're talking to two or more people.</p></div>

<div class="_3e8fY _2povu" itemprop="text"><ul>
<li>What is eating the girls? = Что ест де́вочек? (this is usually used non-metaphorically, i.e. some monster is literally eating them; if you want to ask why they are sad, you could say Почему́ де́вочки грустя́т? 'Why are the girls sad?')</li>
<li>What are the girls eating? = Что едя́т де́вочки?</li>
</ul>
<p>The difference is:</p>
<ul>
<li>Едят is a plural verb form, ест is a singular verb form. When что is a subject, you need to use the singular verb form. When де́вочки is a subject, you need to use the plural verb form.</li>
<li>Девочки is a nominative case (used for the subject), де́вочек is accusative (used for the object).</li>
</ul></div>

<div class="_3e8fY _2povu" itemprop="text"><p>Well, meaning? «Ест» is '(he/she/it) eats', «идёт» is '(he/she/it) goes' (mainly on foot).</p>
<p>Did you mean to ask about the difference between «ест» '(he/she/it) eats' and «едя́т» '(they) eat'? Russian verbs have 6 forms: for 2 numbers and 3 persons:</p>
<ul>
<li>я е́м 'I eat',</li>
<li>ты е́шь 'you eat' (informal singular),</li>
<li>он ест 'he eats', она́ ест 'she eats', оно́ 'it eats', ко́шка ест 'cat eats', па́нда ест 'panda eats' (used with all singular nouns),</li>
<li>мы еди́м 'we eat',</li>
<li>вы еди́те 'you eat' (plural or polite),</li>
<li>они́ едя́т 'they eat', ко́шки едя́т 'cats eat', па́нды едя́т 'pandas eat' (used with all plural nouns).</li>
</ul></div>

<p>In the end of the word, д and т are not distinguished, both are devoiced to /t/. (The same is true for б/п, в/ф, г/к, ж/ш, з/с).</p>

<div class="_3e8fY _2povu" itemprop="text"><p>Because «ко́шки» is plural, and you can't usually translate a plural noun with a singular.</p>
<p>It might be slighly complicated because Russian nouns have several cases. In Nominative case, singular is ко́шка and plural is ко́шки. Nominative case is used for someone doing the action, the subject:</p>
<ul>
<li>На́ши ко́шки едя́т я́йца. 'Our cats eat eggs.'</li>
<li>На́ша ко́шка ест я́йца. 'Our cat eats eggs.'</li>
</ul>
<p>But in genitive case, ко́шки is singualr and ко́шек is plural. Genitive is used to express possession (следы́ ко́шки 'cat's footprints') or with «нет» to express absence or not having:</p>
<ul>
<li>У нас нет ко́шки. 'We don't have a cat.'</li>
<li>У нас нет ко́шек. 'We don't have cats.'</li>
</ul>
<p>So, ко́шки can be both nominative singular, or genitive plural. You'll eventually learn which constructions require genitive, and which require nominative, and won't confuse these two.</p></div>
